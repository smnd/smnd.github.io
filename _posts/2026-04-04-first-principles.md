Most people use first principles as a phrase, not a practice. It gets dropped into strategy discussions as shorthand for "I thought hard about this." But thinking from first principles is a specific thing, and it is harder than it sounds.

It means starting from the most fundamental truths of a problem. Not best practices. Not what worked last time. The actual atomic beginnings underneath.

This is a bottom-up approach. You break the problem down until you reach statements that are either provably true or are assumptions you are willing to accept. Then you build back up. In scientific fields, those foundations tend to be laws. In everything involving people, they tend to be assumptions. That distinction matters.

The reason this is worth the effort is not that it always produces better answers. Sometimes you rebuild from the ground up and arrive at exactly the same solution everyone else uses. But now you understand *why* that solution is true. And that is fundamentally different from just knowing *that* it is true.

Knowing *why* gives you the ability to adapt when conditions change. Knowing *that* gives you nothing when the environment shifts.

As Feynman put it:

>"I don't know what's the matter with people: they don't learn by understanding; they learn by some other way, by rote or something. Their knowledge is so fragile!"

Fragile knowledge is everywhere. A product team copies a feature because a successful competitor has it, without understanding the conditions that make it work. It is not a stupid decision. It is a decision built on borrowed understanding. It might work until it doesn't.

Here is an example. When you are deciding how to structure a payment flow in a consumer app, the conventional approach is to look at what Grab, PayPal, or whoever the local leader does, and adapt it. That is reasoning by analogy. First principles would ask: what does this specific user need to feel confident enough to complete this transaction? What information reduces their uncertainty? What friction is genuinely protective versus just inherited from a desktop web flow that made sense in 2012? You might arrive at the same four-step checkout. But you might not. And if you do, you will know which steps to cut when the context changes.

The honest caveat: first-principles thinking is expensive. It demands time, effort, and iteration. You cannot apply it to every decision. Most decisions do not deserve it. The skill is knowing which ones do.

When it comes down to it, everything that is not a law of nature is just a shared belief. As Yuval Noah Harari argues in "Sapiens", the entire infrastructure of human civilisation - money, nations, corporations, laws - runs on shared beliefs that we collectively agree to treat as real. First principles is the practice of noticing which of your beliefs are borrowed and deciding whether to keep them.

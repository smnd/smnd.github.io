Some questions are hard to answer directly. How do I be happy? How do I build a good product? How do I make better decisions? These are paralysing questions because the answer space is enormous.

Inversion makes them easier. Instead of asking what to do, ask what to avoid.

How do I be happy? Hard to answer. How do I be unhappy? Much easier. Cling to the past. Maintain relationships that drain you. Refuse to accept that people are flawed. Set expectations so high that reality can never meet them. Now you have a clear list. Avoid those things and you are closer to the answer than any direct pursuit of happiness would get you.

This works because identifying the negative is almost always easier than identifying the positive. Sun Tzu's principle was that the skilled fighter wins by making no mistakes.

The same applies to decisions. Instead of asking "what should I invest in?", ask "what are the most common investment mistakes?" Understanding those mistakes will filter out bad decisions before you even start looking for good ones.

There is a second, less obvious way to use inversion. Instead of avoiding failure, you assume the conclusion you want to reach and then work backwards to figure out what else must be true for that conclusion to hold.

Say you believe a new feature will increase retention. Instead of arguing forward from the idea to the outcome, invert it. If this feature does increase retention, what else must be true? Users must discover it. They must understand it without explanation. It must solve a recurring problem. The value must compound with repeated use. Now you have a list of assumptions you can test. If any of them fail, the conclusion probably fails too, regardless of how good the idea sounds.

The first form says "avoid the bad." The second says "stress-test the good." Both are useful. The second is rarer in practice because it requires you to challenge your own ideas, which most people would rather not do. The trap is spending so long cataloguing what to avoid that you never commit to a direction. Inversion is a tool for clarifying choices, not a substitute for making them.
